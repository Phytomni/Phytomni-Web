**Title**: Advances in Single-Cell RNA Sequencing (scRNA-seq) Methodology and Its Applications in Plant Biology

**Results**:

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) methodology in plant biology

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has revolutionized the field of plant biology by enabling the study of gene expression at the individual cell level, thus addressing the limitations of bulk tissue sampling which often masks cellular heterogeneity [1]. The application of protoplasts in scRNA-seq has been particularly transformative, allowing for comprehensive exploration of single-cell transcriptomes and providing insights into plant development, responses to environmental cues, and crop improvement [1]. For instance, Xie et al. developed a protocol for rice chloroplast protoplasts and identified key regulatory genes through scRNA-seq, highlighting the potential of this approach in understanding complex biological processes [1]. Similarly, Li et al. utilized scRNA-seq to study wood formation, establishing high-resolution expression profiles that offer invaluable insights into the intricate processes underpinning this developmental pathway [1]. These studies underscore the power of scRNA-seq in dissecting transcriptional heterogeneity among cells, thereby expanding our understanding of cellular diversity and paving the way for breakthroughs in plant biology and developmental studies [1][2].

The technological advancements in single-cell transcriptomics have broadened the scope of plant biotechnology research, enabling detailed analysis of gene expression and elucidating complex cellular processes and developmental pathways [2]. Despite the challenges associated with sample preparation, cell isolation, and data complexity, strategies such as optimizing protoplast isolation and improving computational tools for data integration are being developed to mitigate these issues [2]. The practical applications of single-cell transcriptomics span from improving crop traits through precise genetic modifications to enhancing our understanding of plant-microbe interactions, thereby contributing to sustainable agriculture [2]. The development of FACS-free single-nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq) has further advanced plant research by overcoming the limitations of protoplast isolation, demonstrating high-quality data and utility in cell type classification and marker gene identification [3]. This approach has revealed additional cell clusters that were undetected by traditional scRNA-seq, highlighting its potential in uncovering new cellular dynamics [3].

Single-cell RNA sequencing has also provided a high-resolution roadmap for understanding the multicellular compartmentation of specialized metabolism, as demonstrated in C. roseus leaves [4]. By optimizing the scRNA-seq procedure, researchers have been able to dissect the compartmentation of specialized metabolism at the single-cell level, facilitating the development of methods to increase the yields of valuable metabolites [4]. The success of scRNA-seq in various plants, including Arabidopsis, rice, and maize, suggests a promising future for single-cell transcriptomics in plant research, with the potential to exponentially increase our understanding of gene expression at the single-cell level [5][6]. The high-resolution atlas of cellular characterization provided by droplet-based scRNA-seq has vastly improved our understanding of cellular interactions and microenvironmental influences, although challenges in protoplast isolation remain [6][7]. High-throughput single-cell RNA sequencing in Arabidopsis roots has demonstrated the technology's sensitivity and reproducibility, further validating its application in plant research [8].

Cell-type-specific responses to biotic stresses in plants

Understanding cell-type-specific responses to biotic stresses in plants is crucial for developing resilient crops. Single-cell transcriptomics has enabled the analysis of molecular responses at the single-cell level, revealing the complexity of plant stress responses and the specific roles of different cell types [9][10]. For example, the analysis of Arabidopsis guard cells in response to abscisic acid has identified 909 genes specifically regulated, highlighting the sensitivity of single-cell transcriptomic approaches [11]. The study of various plant single cell types, such as pollen, trichomes, and root hair cells, has provided insights into plant responses to both biotic and abiotic stresses, revealing new regulatory genes and pathways [10]. The use of single-cell transcriptomics to investigate fungal infection in Arabidopsis has unveiled cell-type-specific gene expression and transcriptional reprogramming, emphasizing the importance of spatially dynamic responses in plant-pathogen interactions [12].

MicroRNAs (miRNAs) play a significant role in the regulatory networks of plant responses to biotic stresses, with scRNA-seq revealing rapid and cell-type-specific miRNA responses under ABA treatment [13]. The identification of miRNA-TF-mRNA regulation modules has provided insights into the hub role of miRNAs in these networks, particularly in mesophyll and vascular cells [13]. The comparative transcriptomic analysis of Nicotiana tabacum trichomes in response to cadmium treatment has demonstrated the potential of single-cell transcriptomics in revealing new regulatory genes in response to heavy metal stress [11]. The integration of single-cell transcriptomics with other omics technologies, such as proteomics and metabolomics, is expected to further broaden our understanding of plant stress responses [14].

The analysis of plant single cell types in response to environmental stresses has revealed significant insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying plant defense. For instance, the barley β-extension EXPB7 gene was transcriptionally regulated in response to rhizobia in root hair cells, highlighting the importance of specific cell types in plant-microbe interactions . The study of Arabidopsis mesophyll cells infected by Hyaloperonospora arabidopsidis has provided valuable data on plant responses to pathogenic microorganisms . The identification of Arabidopsis candidate genes in response to biotic and abiotic stresses using comparative microarrays has highlighted the complex regulatory networks involved in plant stress responses, including transcription factors, kinase cascades, and reactive oxygen species [15]. The integration of single-cell transcriptomics with other omics datasets has the potential to provide a more comprehensive view of plant stress responses and improve crop resistance [16].

Cell-type-specific responses to abiotic stresses in plants

Abiotic stresses, such as drought, salinity, and cold, trigger complex physiological and biochemical changes in plants, which occur in a cell-type-specific manner [17]. Advances in cell-type-specific isolation techniques, such as laser capture microdissection (LCM) and fluorescent activated cell sorting (FACS), have enabled the transcriptional profiling of specific plant cell types, providing insights into their responses to abiotic stresses . For example, the transcriptional response of Arabidopsis roots to various environmental stresses has revealed stress-specific transcriptional profiles, with approximately 25% of cell-type specific genes being stress-specific [18]. The identification of cell-type-specific calcium responses in Arabidopsis roots has highlighted the role of specific cell types in the detection and response to abiotic stimuli, revealing a new level of complexity in root calcium signaling [19][20].

Single-cell transcriptomes have revealed spatiotemporal heat stress responses in maize roots, identifying cell types that display dramatic responses to heat stress and uncovering cell-specific marker genes [21]. The functional analysis of CYP98A8 and CYP98A9 in Arabidopsis has provided insights into the transcriptional stress responses in root cell types, demonstrating the unique stress-specific transcriptional profiles of different cell types [18]. The integration of scRNA-seq data with spatial transcriptomics and chromatin accessibility assays has the potential to unravel the intricate gene regulatory networks orchestrating abiotic stress responses, offering opportunities for genetic engineering to enhance stress resilience in crops [22]. The role of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and nitric oxide (NO) in plant responses to abiotic stresses has been highlighted, with their biosynthesis and metabolism influencing plant response mechanisms [23].

The perception of adverse environmental conditions triggers stress-specific signal transduction, involving various second messengers such as Ca2+ and ROS, which exhibit cell type-specific and stress-specific signatures [24]. The analysis of Arabidopsis root cells in response to nutrient deprivation, salinity, and abscisic acid has provided valuable insights into the transcriptomic profiles of root hair cells and other root cell types, highlighting the importance of cell-type-specific transcriptomes in understanding plant responses to abiotic stresses . The integration of scRNA-seq data with other omics approaches, such as proteomics and metabolomics, is expected to provide a more comprehensive view of plant stress responses and facilitate the development of stress-tolerant crops [25].

Integrative analysis of scRNA-seq data to understand stress responses in plant organs

The integrative analysis of scRNA-seq data with other omics datasets has the potential to provide a comprehensive understanding of plant stress responses at the cellular level. The development of computational frameworks, such as LRT, for the integrative analysis of scRNA-seq and scTCR-seq data has enabled the investigation of clonal differentiation heterogeneity, revealing distinct clonotype clusters with diverse expansion capabilities and gene usage patterns [26][27]. The integration of scRNA-seq data with GWAS results has pinpointed specific liver cell types relevant to blood lipid levels, demonstrating the potential of integrative analyses in identifying cell types relevant to stress responses [28]. The application of machine learning techniques to RNA-Seq data has identified key biomarkers associated with resistance to Rhizoctonia solani in sugar beet, highlighting the effectiveness of integrative approaches in biomarker discovery [29].

The transcriptome meta-analysis of Arabidopsis thaliana has identified candidate hub genes and pathways involved in pathogen stress responses, providing insights into the common transcriptional regulation of plant-pathogen interactions [30]. The integrative analysis of bulk RNA-seq and scRNA-seq data has indicated the prognostic and immunologic values of SERPINH1 in glioma, demonstrating the potential of integrative approaches in understanding disease mechanisms [31]. The adaptively weighted multi-view learning framework (scAWMV) for the integrative analysis of parallel scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq data has provided biologically meaningful low-dimensional representations, enhancing our understanding of cellular heterogeneity [32].

The application of single-cell RNA sequencing in plant research has revolutionized our understanding of plant growth, development, and stress responses. The integration of scRNA-seq data with other omics datasets, such as metabolomics and proteomics, has provided profound insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying plant stress responses and facilitated the development of stress-tolerant crops [33]. The construction of single-cell transcriptional atlases in maize roots has revealed cell-type-specific responses to heat stress, offering insights into the cellular mechanisms underlying heat tolerance . The meta-analysis of RNA-Seq data across different crops has identified key genes and chromosome regions linked to drought responses, providing valuable information for crop improvement [34].

**Conclusion**:

The advancements in single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) methodology have significantly enhanced our understanding of plant biology, particularly in the study of gene expression at the individual cell level. The application of scRNA-seq in plant research has provided high-resolution insights into cellular heterogeneity, developmental processes, and stress responses, paving the way for breakthroughs in plant biotechnology and crop improvement. The integration of scRNA-seq data with other omics datasets and computational frameworks has the potential to unravel the intricate gene regulatory networks orchestrating plant responses to biotic and abiotic stresses, offering opportunities for genetic engineering and the development of resilient crops. The future of plant research lies in the continued integration of single-cell transcriptomics with other technologies, providing a comprehensive view of plant biology and facilitating the development of sustainable agricultural practices.
